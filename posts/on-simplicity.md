Every few months I reread the same handful of essays about software. They're not about frameworks or languages. They're about the discipline of leaving things out.

## The subtraction principle

The hardest decisions in programming aren't about what to add. They're about what not to add. Features that seem obviously necessary in week one become the code you regret in year two.

## What this looks like in practice

A function that does one thing. A module with a clear boundary. A system you can hold entirely in your head. These aren't signs of laziness — they're signs of hard work.

## The cost of complexity

Every abstraction you introduce is a debt. Sometimes it's worth it. Often it isn't. The question to ask isn't "could this be useful?" but "is the simplification worth the indirection?"
